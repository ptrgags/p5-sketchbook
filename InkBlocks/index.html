Ink Blocks (2024)

A silly automaton inspired by fountain pens and sunflowers. Let me explain.

In my fountain pen case, I keep 6 fountain pens for journaling and drawing so I can always have a variety of colors available.

I treat the fountain pen case like a circular buffer. Each day I use a pen from the next slot, circling back to the start when I reach the end.

Eventually, a pen runs out of ink. I remove it from the pen case for cleaning. The other pens shift over to fill the gap, leaving slots open at the right end of the pen case.

When I clean and refill pens, I choose a new color to keep things interesting, and I add it to one of those slots in the end.

The question then is, "If I repeat this process for many months, what pattern is produced?". So I made this little automaton to simulate the above process.

Each row of 6 blocks in the above visualization represents the colors used in a span of 6 days (one traversal of the fountain pen case). The hue represents the ink color, while the decreasing brightness represents using up the ink over time. Each successive row is the new set of colors after removing empty pens from the middle of the case, and adding refilled pens at the end with new colors.

The result is the stripey texture you see above. The stripes have a slant to them because new pens appear on the right, and shift towards the left as pens run out of ink. Furthermore, colors are brightest on the right since new pens are at full capacity, and darkest on the left because those pens have been used a number of times.

What has this to do with sunflowers? I needed a color palette where each new entry contrasts the previous few colors. This was around the same time I was exploring the math of sunflower spirals. And I realized that spiral phyllotaxis would be perfect for this. That's why I made Phyllotaxis Palette.
