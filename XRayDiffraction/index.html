X-Ray Diffraction (2026-04)

I was talking to someone who works in a materials-science related field, and he was telling me about X-ray diffraction to study the structure of crystals. The math details got me curious enough to make this visualization. This is an early proof-of-concept.

The top half of the screen shows the lab setup. The crystal is suspended on a goniometer which can rotate the crystal to a desired orientation. An x-ray source emits a beam of a constant wavelength at the crystal. When the crystal is oriented at certain angles, the scattered x-rays will constructively interfere. In this visualization, the angled beams that flash briefly are these peaks.

Note: In real life, the beams would not be visible since human eyes cannot see x-rays.

The bottom half is a graph of the spatial frequencies of both the crystal and the x-rays. Everything in this graph is measured in cycles/angstrom in the x and y direction. There are several details visualized here:

  • The red dots are the reciprocal lattice of the crystal. Each set of parallel planes through the atoms of the crystal corresponds to a wavevector g_hk. As the crystal rotates, so does the reciprocal lattice.
  • The purple vector pointing to the right represents the spatial frequency of the incoming x-ray. In other words, k_in = 1/wavelength in the +x direction.
  • The flashing purple vectors pointing at diagonals to the right are spatial frequencies k_out of the detectable outgoing x-rays
  • The purple circle represents the possible differences between the incoming and outgoing wavevectors, k_out - k_in. This is referred to as Ewald's Sphere.
  • The flashing yellow vectors on the left show the intersections of the reciprocal lattice and Ewald's sphere. These points represent spatial frequencies where constructive interference (and thus a detectable k_out) occur.

Note: My visualization uses linear spatial frequencies in cycles/angstrom. Much of the literature uses angular spatial frequencies in radians/angstrom. The only difference is a scale factor of 2pi.

There's one important subtlety here: For most angles, the lattice doesn't exactly intersect Ewald's sphere, and thus no constructive interference happens! This makes visualization tricky.

In real life, time is continuous, so as you rotate the crystal through various angles, a lattice point will intersect the sphere at some instant in time when it crosses from outside to inside or vice-versa. However, in computer animation we're computing discrete snapshots in time, so in many cases the lattice points will jump across the sphere without ever intersecting it. This means there should be several more peaks than what is currently shown.

This is an area of this sketch that needs improvement - I need to handle diffraction peaks that happen in between animation frames. I'm still thinking about how best to visualize this. Stay tuned!
