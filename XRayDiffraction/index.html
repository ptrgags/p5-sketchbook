X-Ray Diffraction (2026-04)

I was talking to someone who works in a materials-science related field, and he was telling me about X-ray diffraction to study the structure of crystals. The math details got me curious enough to make this visualization. This is an early proof-of-concept.

The top half of the screen shows the lab setup. The crystal is suspended on a goniometer which can rotate the crystal to a desired orientation. An x-ray source emits a beam of a constant wavelength at the crystal. When the crystal is oriented at certain angles, the scattered x-rays will constructively interfere. In this visualization, the angled beams that flash briefly are these peaks.

Note: In real life, the beams would not be visible since human eyes cannot see x-rays.

The bottom half is a graph of the spatial frequencies both of the crystal and the x-rays. Both axes use units of cycles/angstrom (where an angstrom is 10^-10 m). The x-axis represents spatial frequency in the horizontal direction, and likewise the y-axis represents the vertical spatial frequency. The red points together are the reciprocal lattice of the crystal. Here I'm assuming the simplest case of a cubic lattice with atoms spaced 1 angstrom apart. This means the reciprocal lattice looks similar with vectors spaced 1 cycle/angstrom apart. As the crystal rotates, the orientation of the spatial frequencies rotate in the same direction. This is why the red dots are rotating in the diagram.

Note: My visualization uses linear spatial frequencies in cycles/angstrom. Much of the literature uses angular spatial frequencies in radians/angstrom. The only difference is a scale factor of 2pi.

In this setup, the incoming x-ray beam is always pointed horizontally, so in frequency space, its wavevector shows up as a point on the x-axis (specifically at 1/wavelength in cycles/angstrom). The scattered rays can be in potentially any direction, but since this is elastic scattering, the outgoing ray must have the same energy, and therefore same magnitude of spatial frequency as the input beam. This means you can imagine a sphere in frequency space centered on the incoming beam's wavevector and with a radius that matches the incoming ray. This is called Ewald's Sphere. In this 2D visualization, this sphere shows up as the purple circle. You can think of it as the sphere of possible output directions.

Though the x-rays are bouncing in all directions, most of the time the bounces from different planes of the crystal lattice will result in destructive interference and the result will have a low amplitude. Only outgoing wavevectors corresponding to the reciprocal lattice vectors will be in phase and thus produce an intense peak. In terms of the diagram, this happens whenever a lattice point intersects Ewald's sphere. In the diagram, the flashing lines highlight when this happens.

Here we have a big caveat in visualizing this math: For most orientations of the crystal, the lattice will only intersect the lattice at the origin! Here, the difference between real life (where time is continuous) and animation (where time happens in discrete snapshots) makes a difference. In real life, as you rotate the crystal, every lattice point that crosses the sphere surface will produce a detectable peak, if only for a split second. In an animation, however, it's very easy for the lattice points to skip over the sphere surface from one frame to the next without ever intersecting it. In other words, there should be more peaks in the visualization than shown! This is one area of this sketch that needs improvement, I need to handle diffraction peaks that happen between frames somehow. I'm still thinking about how I want to visualize this.
